1. The 50-30-20 Rule for Budgeting

This popular rule helps allocate your monthly income effectively into three categories:

  • 50% for Needs: These are non-negotiable expenses required for your survival. Examples:
    • Housing (rent or home loan EMI)
    • Groceries and utilities (electricity, water, internet, etc.)
    • Transportation (fuel, public transport, etc.)
    • Insurance premiums
  • 30% for Wants: These are discretionary expenses that improve your lifestyle and bring joy. Examples:
    • Dining out
    • Entertainment subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, etc.)
    • Travel and vacations
    • Shopping for non-essential items
  • 20% for Savings and Investments: This portion builds your financial future and safeguards against emergencies. Examples:
    • Emergency fund contributions
    • SIPs (Systematic Investment Plans) in mutual funds
    • Investments in PPF (Public Provident Fund) or NPS (National Pension System)
    • Prepayment of loans

Why Use This Rule? It simplifies budgeting and ensures you balance living for today with preparing for the future.

2. The FIRE Number (Financial Independence, Retire Early)

FIRE is a movement aimed at achieving financial independence and retiring early by saving aggressively and investing smartly.

How to Calculate Your FIRE Number

Determine Your Annual Expenses: Total your monthly expenses and multiply by 12. Be sure to include all recurring expenses such as:

  • Rent or home maintenance
  • Groceries
  • Utilities
  • Entertainment
  • Insurance premiums

Adjust for Inflation and Lifestyle Changes: Consider that costs will increase with inflation (assume 5–7% annually in India). Add a buffer for medical expenses and lifestyle upgrades.

Apply the 25x Rule: Multiply your annual expenses by 25 to calculate the corpus needed to sustain yourself using a 4% annual withdrawal rate.

Example:

  • Monthly Expenses: ₹50,000
  • Annual Expenses: ₹50,000 × 12 = ₹6,00,000
  • FIRE Number: ₹6,00,000 × 25 = ₹1.5 crore

Strategies to Achieve FIRE:

  • Save 50–70% of your income.
  • Invest aggressively in equity and balanced mutual funds.
  • Avoid lifestyle inflation by keeping discretionary expenses low.

3. The 100 Minus Age Rule for Asset Allocation

This rule helps determine the ideal allocation of your investment portfolio between equity (stocks or mutual funds) and debt (fixed-income instruments):

  • Equity Investments: Higher risk, higher returns. Suitable for younger individuals.
  • Debt Investments: Lower risk, stable returns. Provides security as you age.

How It Works

Subtract your age from 100. The result is the percentage of your portfolio that should be in equities. The remainder should be allocated to debt instruments.

Example for a 30-Year-Old:

  • Equity: 100 - 30 = 70%
  • Debt: 30%

Flexibility: You can adjust based on risk tolerance. For aggressive investors, use “110 minus age” to increase equity exposure.

4. Emergency Fund Rule

An emergency fund is a financial safety net to cover unexpected expenses like:

  • Job loss
  • Medical emergencies
  • Car or home repairs

How Much to Save?

Save 3–6 months’ worth of essential living expenses. Example: If your monthly essential expenses are ₹40,000, your emergency fund should be:

  • Minimum: ₹1,20,000 (3 months)
  • Maximum: ₹2,40,000 (6 months)

Where to Save It?

Keep this fund in liquid and low-risk investments for easy access:

  • High-yield savings accounts
  • Fixed deposits with premature withdrawal options
  • Liquid mutual funds

5. The 4% Rule for Safe Withdrawals

The 4% rule ensures you can withdraw from your retirement corpus without exhausting it during your lifetime.

How It Works

Withdraw 4% of your total investment corpus annually for living expenses. The corpus is invested in a balanced portfolio to grow enough to keep up with inflation.

Example:

  • Retirement Corpus: ₹2 crore
  • Annual Withdrawal: ₹2 crore × 4% = ₹8,00,000
  • Monthly Withdrawal: ₹8,00,000 ÷ 12 = ₹66,667

Why It Works: Historical data shows that a well-balanced portfolio (60% equity, 40% debt) can sustain withdrawals at this rate for over 30 years.
